Assessment

Every client interaction is a discovery opportunity. This chapter gives you a repeatable, structured framework for conducting the initial office space enquiry call — from the first word to the confirmed next step.

The three-part structure works for any city, any client type, and any enquiry size. Master it and you will never walk away from a call without understanding what the client actually needs.

01

The 3-Part Call

A successful client call has three phases. Each phase has a distinct purpose and a time budget. Together they take 10–15 minutes.

Introduction — 30–45 seconds

Open strong. Use the client's name first. Establish who you are, earn the right to ask questions, and set a shared purpose for the call. This is where trust is either built or lost before discovery begins.

Conversation — 4–5 minutes

Discover the real requirement by asking across four themes: Situation, People, Location, and Requirements. Listen more than you talk. This is not an interview or question-answer session — it's a conversation. The details you uncover here determine the quality of your recommendation.

Summary — 1–2 minutes

Read back what you heard. Confirm seat count, location, configuration, budget, and timeline. Commit to a specific next step before ending the call. Never hang up without a clear follow-up.

Most calls are lost in the first 30 seconds or the last 2 minutes. A weak opening loses trust before discovery starts. A missing summary leaves the client without a next step.
02

You Should Know Before the Call

Two minutes of research before you dial changes your entire opening. Do this on the lead form, LinkedIn, or the company website.

Pre-Call Checklist

Company name — Lead form or CRM. Don't mispronounce or misread it.

What the company does — Website or LinkedIn. One sentence is enough: "they're a fintech startup," "they're a logistics firm."

Current office space — Google Maps or LinkedIn. Knowing their current setup tells you the scale of the move and any pain points.

Employee strength & size of organisation

  • Employee strength
  • Size of organisation — Listed / MNC (where is the headquarters?)

Competitor — Might you have been able to cater to some of their competitors?

Profile of client — LinkedIn profile of the person you're calling. Their role, seniority, and background shapes how you pitch.

03

Introduction

The recommended opening script. Every line has a purpose. Do not skip any part of it — each element earns something specific from the client.

The Script

"Hi [Name]!" — Name first, always. It signals that this is a personal call, not a mass dial. It is the first impression.

"Good morning — this is [Your Name] from myHQ by Anarock. I'm calling regarding your office space requirement — is this a good time to talk for 2–3 minutes?" — Identify yourself, state the reason immediately, and ask permission before launching. A client who says "yes" is already more engaged than one who wasn't asked.

"Quick background — we help organizations with their office space needs across all geographies. We work with 5000+ workspace partners and have assisted clients like Rebel Foods, Russell Tobin, JioSaavn, Angel One, Philip Morris, and Zomato in finding the right office solutions." — Name-drop at scale. These are brands the client recognises. This earns instant credibility before a single question is asked.

"We charge zero brokerage." — The biggest barrier to opening up is fear of cost. Remove it in one sentence.

"The objective of this call is to help you through this process." — Frame the call as helping them, not selling to them. This resets the dynamic entirely.

Name first. Always. It is the first impression.

What You Need to Know Before the Call

Situation

  • Why are they looking for an office? (Client nearby / current office bad / expansion / new setup / tenure finished / cost cutting)

End User

  • Who will use the office?
  • Kind of employees
  • Front facing / backend, clients visiting

Location

  • Where do the employees travel from?
  • Where do the founders travel from?
  • Mode of transportation — metro or personal vehicles?
  • What is the location expectation?

Configuration & Cashflow

  • Configuration — seats, cabins, meeting rooms, carpet area?
  • What is the budget expectation? (Monthly cashflow — total spend the business can absorb)
Budget rule: if the client says ₹8K/seat — show options from ₹7K to ₹12K. Budget = 8–15% of monthly salary cost per employee. Never limit your search to their stated number alone.

Others

  • What is the future growth plan?
  • When do they start?
  • Who is the decision maker?
  • Parking
  • Hours of operations
  • Are they already in touch with someone / have they looked around?
05

Summary & Next Steps

Before you end the call, read back everything you heard. This builds trust, catches misunderstandings early, and creates a mutual commitment to next steps.

Summary Template

"Perfect — you're looking for [X seats] in [location], with [configuration]. Monthly budget around ₹[X total / ₹X per seat]. Key requirements: [parking / metro access / 24×7]. Moving in by [date / month]."

Send this summary to the client in writing after the call.

Next Steps Script

"As the next step, I'll identify options that match your requirement and share them with you today."

"Quoted prices typically have 10–15% negotiation room — I'll factor that in."

"Once you've reviewed the options, we can organise site visits together."

Always communicate over company email. Ask: "It would be great if you can share your professional email ID — that helps me generate the best options via our internal system."
Use the client's name during the conversation — not just at the start. A name used naturally mid-call signals you're engaged, not reading from a script.
